**Embedding Islamic Values in Agile Delivery**

## What is Ihsan Agile?

Ihsan Agile is a values-centred guide for Muslim-led teams and organisations using Agile methods.

It explores how Islamic ethical principles drawn from the Qur'an and Sunnah can be made *operational* within everyday Agile events, such as planning, review, and retrospective, without replacing existing frameworks such as Scrum, Kanban, or SAFe.

Rather than introducing a new methodology, Ihsan Agile offers an **ethical overlay**: a way of surfacing intention (*niyyah*), excellence with God-consciousness (*iḥsān*), responsibility (*amānah*), consultation (*shūrā*), public benefit (*maṣlaḥah*), and truthfulness (*ṣidq*) at the points where delivery decisions are actually made.

Version 1.2 extends the framework by adding *ṣidq* (truthfulness) as a Fourth Pillar, making honest communication and technical disclosure a structural foundation, not an optional practice. It also clarifies when to seek Shariah consultation, positions psychological safety as an Islamic requirement for practising *ṣidq*, and expands modular adoption guidance.

The guide is practice-oriented and intentionally modular. Teams can try a single practice, adopt one role, or implement the complete framework — whatever serves their context. No comprehensive adoption required.

## Four Pillars

1. **Niyyah** (نِيَّة) — Intention: Clarify purpose and higher aim before building
2. **Iḥsān** (إِحْسَان) — Excellence with God-consciousness: Work as if Allah sees every detail
3. **Maṣlaḥah** (مَصْلَحَة) — Public Good: Orient toward genuine benefit, not output for its own sake
4. **Ṣidq** (صِدْق) — Truthfulness: Communicate honestly about capabilities, constraints, and limitations, recognising that disclosure builds trust whilst concealment creates harm *(added in v1.2)*

## Five Core Principles

1. **Taqwā** — God-consciousness in all actions
2. **Amānah & Khilāfah** — Stewardship and trusteeship
3. **Shūrā** — Consultation and collective wisdom
4. **ʿIbādah & ʿAdl** — Service as worship and justice
5. **Tazkiyah** — Continuous growth through reflection

## Key Practices

The Ihsan Agile Guide articulates **eight core practices** designed to integrate Islamic ethical reasoning into existing Agile ways of working, with particular emphasis on intention, disclosure, stewardship, and responsibility. They are intentionally **non-prescriptive** and may be adapted to different contexts.

1. **Niyyah Check-ins**
Making intention explicit at moments of commitment, prioritisation, and delivery, particularly where trade-offs are accepted.

2. **Ethical Completeness**
Extending notions of "done" to include justice, disclosure, and responsibility, not only functional or technical criteria.

3. **Technical Disclosure Management** *(expanded in v1.1, deepened in v1.2)*
Actively identifying and communicating known technical, organisational, or ethical uncertainties that may affect users or stakeholders. Reframes "technical debt" as "technical disclosure": constraints disclosed to those affected, not managed as hidden internal liabilities.

4. **Shūrā as a Decision Practice**
Structuring consultation around consequential decisions, especially where impacts are uneven, unclear, or deferred.

5. **Stewardship and Mīzān**
Attending to balance, sustainability, and long-term consequences across systems, people, and obligations.

6. **Muhāsabah Retrospectives**
Reviewing outcomes, intentions, and consequences — including ethical and stakeholder impact — as part of continuous improvement.

7. **Stakeholder Barakah Reviews** *(introduced in v1.1)*
Genuine consultation with those affected by technical decisions, assessing whether work created real uplift and whether constraints were disclosed honestly.

8. **Continuous Niyyah Alignment**
Regularly reconnecting day-to-day work with higher purpose, particularly as scope, pressure, or incentives shift.

## Roles

### Ihsan Agile Facilitator (IAF)

Embeds ethical consciousness into how teams work — enriching Sprint Planning with Niyyah Check-ins, Retrospectives with Muhāsabah reflection, and Definition of Done with ethical completeness criteria. May be taken on by an existing team member (e.g. Scrum Master, coach, or rotating facilitator).

### Ihsan Agile Product Steward (IAPS) *(introduced in v1.1)*

Extends Islamic values into what teams build and how technical decisions are made. Reframes "technical debt" as "technical disclosure," ensures genuine stakeholder consultation (*shūrā*), prioritises public benefit (*maṣlaḥah*), and maintains transparent stewardship (*amānah* and *ṣidq*) throughout the product lifecycle. Maintains the Technical Uncertainty Register and leads Gharar Assessments.

Both roles may be adopted, adapted, combined, or held lightly. Their usefulness is intentionally left open to evaluation through pilot work and community feedback.

## What's New in Version 1.2

* **Ṣidq as Fourth Pillar** — Truthfulness elevated to foundational framework status alongside niyyah, iḥsān, and maṣlaḥah
* **Psychological safety as Islamic requirement** — Ṣidq requires agency; environments that punish honesty make truthful communication structurally impossible
* **Shariah consultation boundaries clarified** — New section distinguishes operational framework guidance from questions requiring scholarly expertise
* **Refined pillar interdependence** — Updated guidance on how maṣlaḥah (beneficial outcomes) and ṣidq (truthful means) work together
* **Enhanced modularity guidance** — Expanded "What You Don't Need to Do" section; teams can benefit from individual practices without comprehensive implementation

## What Was New in Version 1.1

* **IAPS role** — Addressing critical gaps in conventional product ownership around transparency and stakeholder protection
* **Technical Uncertainty Register (TUR)** — Transforming hidden technical constraints into disclosed commitments
* **Gharar Assessment Checklist** — Evaluating when technical shortcuts require stakeholder disclosure and consent
* **Stakeholder Barakah Reviews** — Genuine consultation with those affected by technical decisions
* **Critical analysis of technical debt** — Showing how conventional frameworks normalise riba-based thinking and create gharar through non-disclosure
